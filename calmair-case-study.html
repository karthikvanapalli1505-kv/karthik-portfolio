Calm Air

A step-by-step guidance platform for first-time international students arriving in the UK — replacing 12+ scattered pages with one calm, structured journey.

Role
Product Designer (Solo)
Duration
3 Weeks
Platform
Desktop Web
Tools
Figma · FigJam
Type
End-to-End Design
The Problem

First-time arrival
is overwhelming

International students arriving in the UK for the first time face a series of critical steps — passport control, biometric verification, baggage, customs, transport — in rapid succession, in an unfamiliar environment, often after a 10+ hour flight. The information exists, but it is scattered across 12+ government, university, and airline websites.

Students land in a high-stress situation without a single reliable source of truth. Calm Air was designed to fix this — a 9-level progressive guidance platform that walks students through every step of UK airport arrival, one screen at a time.

Story / Context

Where the
gap was found

The insight came directly from personal experience arriving in the UK as an international student. The formal guidance was scattered, inconsistent, and assumed prior knowledge. The unofficial guidance — YouTube videos, Reddit threads, university Facebook groups — was helpful but unreliable and hard to navigate under pressure.

The real gap was not missing information. It was missing structure. Students needed a guided sequence, not a search engine. Calm Air provides that structure — progressive disclosure at each stage, showing only what is relevant right now.

Research

Three key
user insights

01
Information overload causes paralysis
Students shown all arrival steps simultaneously reported higher anxiety than those given one step at a time. Progressive disclosure — showing only the current step — reduces cognitive load at the exact moment it matters most.
02
Progress visibility reduces anxiety
Knowing "I am at step 4 of 9" provides control. Students reported that a visible progress tracker transformed the arrival experience from "unknown duration chaos" to "a managed process with an end in sight."
03
Document prep anxiety starts before the airport
Many students were unsure which documents to carry and in what order to present them. A document preparation section before Level 1 was added to address this — reducing uncertainty before the journey even begins.
Information Architecture

How the platform
is structured

The IA is a linear nine-level hierarchy mapping the exact physical journey through a UK airport — from landing gate to exit. Each level corresponds to one real-world location or checkpoint. Users cannot skip levels, ensuring they never arrive at a checkpoint unprepared.

User Flow

The complete
arrival journey

The main flow maps the student from document prep through all nine physical checkpoints to final exit. Each level unlocks only when the previous level is marked complete — enforcing the linear journey that mirrors the real airport process.

Design Decisions

Why these
choices were made

Why a linear locked flow — not free navigation?
Free navigation creates decision fatigue at the worst possible moment. The airport journey is physically linear — the product mirrors that reality. Unlocking levels in sequence means students are always prepared for where they are, not lost in what comes later.
Why progressive disclosure per level?
Showing all nine levels simultaneously caused anxiety in early testing. Each level shows only the information relevant to that checkpoint — one action, one context. Complexity is managed by revealing it gradually.
Why a document prep section before Level 1?
Testing showed students were anxious about documents before they even reached the gate. A pre-arrival document checklist removed that anxiety at source — turning an unknown into a confirmed, ready state before the physical journey began.
Reflection & Next Steps

What I learned

Reflection

Calm Air taught me that structure itself is a form of care. When a product removes uncertainty at a stressful moment, the user feels supported — even if the design is invisible.

The hardest design decision was what to exclude. Every level could have included more information. The discipline was in showing the minimum viable guidance at each step.

Next Steps
  • Mobile version for in-airport use on phone
  • Airport-specific variants (Heathrow T2, T5, Gatwick, etc.)
  • Offline mode for areas with poor airport WiFi
  • University integration — pre-arrival email link
  • Multilingual support — Hindi, Mandarin, Arabic
Outcome
Outcome
  • Simplified the core user flow to reduce cognitive load during key tasks
  • Improved hierarchy and clarity across primary screens
  • Designed error and edge states to maintain user progress during failures
What I Learned
  • Designing edge cases early prevents usability issues later in development
  • Clear information architecture often improves usability more than visual changes alone
