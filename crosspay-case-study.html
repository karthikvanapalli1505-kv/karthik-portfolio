CrossPay

An international money transfer app designed for Indian students in the UK — built on trust, transparency, and a clear six-step flow.

The Problem

Sending money abroad
feels risky

Indian students in the UK frequently send money home — for family, emergencies, or savings. Existing apps serve the need technically, but first-time international senders face a trust problem: real fees are hidden until deep in the flow, entering bank details feels unsafe, and there is no reassurance when something goes wrong.

CrossPay was designed to fix this — showing real costs upfront, providing a clear step-by-step journey, and building confidence at every screen before a single pound moves.

Story / Context

Where the
problem was found

The insight came from personal experience and conversations with other Indian students in the UK. Most had sent money at least once, and almost all described the same pattern: started in one app, got anxious about fees, switched to another, and often gave up entirely.

The apps weren't broken — the experience was designed for confident, frequent users. First-time international senders needed something built specifically for them: honest, reassuring, and clear from the first screen to the receipt.

Research

Three key
user insights

01
Hidden fees destroy trust
Benchmarking Wise, PayPal and Remitly showed fees hidden late in the flow were the single biggest drop-off cause. Users want to see the real cost before entering any personal information.
02
First-time senders need reassurance
Students sending internationally for the first time described anxiety about security, regulation, and delivery. FCA badges, rate lock timers, and confirmation screens directly address this.
03
Error recovery matters most
Failed transfers that cleared form data caused the most frustration. Preserving entered details and showing a clear retry path is a critical trust signal — not a nice-to-have.
Information Architecture

How the app
is structured

The IA is built around a single primary action — sending money — with everything else as supporting structure. No more than three taps from any screen to initiate a transfer.

User Flow

The complete
transfer journey

Six steps: splash → home → select recipient → enter amount → review → payment. Each step has one clear decision. Simplified from an initial eight-step version after testing showed confidence dropping when steps felt redundant.

Design Decisions

Why these
choices were made

Why show the exchange rate before asking for personal data?
Users abandon when data entry feels unbalanced with visible value. Showing the live rate, fee, and total on the home screen means users commit informed — not blind.
Why keep a dedicated review screen?
International transfers are irreversible. A review screen is not friction — it is reassurance. It gives users one final moment of control before money moves.
Why preserve transfer data after a failed payment?
Clearing form data on error was the highest frustration trigger in testing. The error state keeps all entered details and shows a clear retry path — treating failure as recoverable, not final.
Reflection & Next Steps

What I learned

Reflection

This project taught me that trust in fintech is not about visual style — it is about information timing. The biggest design decisions were about what to show and when, not how it looks.

Competitor benchmarking became a design tool, not just a research step. Mapping Wise, PayPal and Remitly side-by-side revealed specific gaps that became direct design opportunities.

Next Steps
  • Biometric authentication for returning users
  • Scheduled and recurring transfer options
  • Multi-currency support beyond INR
  • Push notifications for transfer status
  • In-app rate alerts when exchange rate improves
Outcome
Outcome
  • Simplified the core user flow to reduce cognitive load during key tasks
  • Improved hierarchy and clarity across primary screens
  • Designed error and edge states to maintain user progress during failures
What I Learned
  • Designing edge cases early prevents usability issues later in development
  • Clear information architecture often improves usability more than visual changes alone
